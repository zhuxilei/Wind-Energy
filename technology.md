                  <ul>

                    <li>Easy to mount at ground level, no tower needed</li>
                    <li>Low rotation speed - no gearbox needed - less noise</li>
                    <li>Generally near the ground with low wind speeds</li>
                    <li>Self-starting problems</li>
                    <li>Drag devices capture only ~15% of energy, Darrieus ~40%.</li>
                    <li>No large-scale commercial application</li>

                Horizontal
                </div>
            </div>
            <div class="panel2_inner_blue">
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                The rotor is parallel to the wind direction. All devices use lift forces rather than drag.</div>
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark"> 
                  <p>Horizontal axis is the most common design for turbines, and is also the design of the traditional wind mills. For electricity generation, mostly 3 blades or less.            </p>

                <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                  <ul>
                    <li>
                      Tip speed ratio is the ratio of the tangential	velocity of the blade (at the tip) to the undisturbed wind speed. Fewer blades means higher tip speed.
                    The optimum tip speed ratio that maximises the lift-to-drag force ratio is around 8. With more than 4 blades, less efficiency because each blade operates in the wake of others.</li>
                    <li>Efficiencies up to 50%</li>
                    <li>Lower cut-in wind speeds than vertical turbines</li>
                  </ul>

    	Axis Orientation and Hub</div>
    </div>
    <div class="panel1_inner_grey">
        <div class="panel2">
            <div class="panel2_header_blue">
                <div class="header_spacer_dark">
                Up-Wind
                </div>
            </div>
      		<div class="panel2_inner_blue">
            	<div class="inner_spacer_dark">Upwind devices have the rotor facing the wind
            		<table width="100%" border="0" cellspacing="0" cellpadding="5">
                  <tr>
                    <td width="44%">
                    	<img src="images/wec_Upwind.jpg" width="150" height="150" />
                    </td>
                    <td width="56%" valign="top"><ul>
                      <li>Avoids the wind shade that the tower causes. Fewer fluctuations in the power output.</li>
                      <li>Requires a rigid hub, which has to be away from the tower. Otherwise, if the blades are bending too far, they will hit the tower. </li>
                      <li>This is the dominant design for most wind turbines in the MW- range</li>
                    </ul></td>
                  </tr>
                </table>

            	
            		
            	</div>
        	</div>

    		</div>

                <div class="header_spacer_dark">
                Down-Wind
                </div>
            </div>
            <div class="panel2_inner_blue">
            	<div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                Downwind devices have the rotor on the lee-side.
           		
                  <table width="100%" border="0" cellspacing="0" cellpadding="5">
                  <tr>
                    <td width="44%">
                    	<img src="images/wec_Downwind.jpg" alt="Down Wind Turbine" width="150" height="150" />
                    </td>
                    <td width="56%" valign="top"><ul>
                      <li>May be built without a yaw mechanism if the nacelle has a streamlined body that will make it follow the wind. </li>
                      <li>Rotor can be more flexible: Blades can bend at high speeds,  taking load off the tower. Allow for lighter build.</li>
                      <li>Increased fluctuations in wind power, as blades are affected by the tower shade.</li>
                      <li>Only small wind turbines.</li>
                    </ul></td>
                  </tr>

<div class="panel1">
	<div class="panel1_header_grey">
    	<div class="header_spacer">
        
    	Control Mechansim</div>
    </div>
    <div class="panel1_inner_grey">
    <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
      <p>A control mechanism is an overspeed control that allows the rotors to be slowed down or stopped. Its purpose is to </p>
      <ul>
        <li>optimize aerodynamic efficiency</li>
        <li>keep the generator with its speed and torque limits and rotor and tower within strength limits</li>
        <li>enable maintenance</li>
        <li>educe noise</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
        <div class="panel3">
            <div class="panel3_header_blue">
                <div class="header_spacer_dark">Stalling</div>
            </div>
  <div class="panel3_inner_blue">
              <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                Principle: Increased angle of attack results in decreasing lift-to-drag ratio. &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
<img src="images/wec_Stall_Control.jpg" alt="Stall Control" width="226" height="185" /> 
                </div>
              <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                <ul>
                  <li> Passive: Blades are at a fixed pitch that starts to stall when the wind speed is too high.</li>
                  <li>Active: motor turns the blades towards stall when wind speeds are too high.</li>
                  <li>Hybrid: Pitch can be adjusted manually to reflect site's particular wind regime.</li>
                  <li>Disadvantage: Stalled blades cause large vibration and therefore noise.</li>
                </ul>
            </div>
            </div>
            <div class="panel3_footer_blue">
            </div>
        </div>
        <div class="panel3">
            <div class="panel3_header_blue">
                <div class="header_spacer_dark">
                Pitch Control
                </div>
            </div>
          <div class="panel3_inner_blue">
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                Principle: Decrease angle of attack also	results in decreasing lif-to-drag ratio.
                </div>
              <img src="images/wec_Pitch_Control.jpg" alt="Pitch Control" width="226" height="185" /> 
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark"> 
                  <p>Always active control: Blades rotate out of the wind when wind speeds are too high.</p>
                  <p>&nbsp;</p>

        <div class="panel3">
            <div class="panel3_header_blue">
                <div class="header_spacer_dark">
                Furling
                </div>
            </div>
            <div class="panel3_inner_blue">
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                Principle: Moving the axis out of the direction of the wind decreases angle of attack and cross-section</div>
                <img src="images/wec_Furling.jpg" alt="Furling" width="227" height="185" /> 
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark"> 
                  <p>Standard modern turbines all furl in high wind.                    </p>
                  <ul>
                    <li>Requires active pitch control: Pitch angle of the blades needs to be minimised first, otherwise the torque on the rotor would be to big for furling. </li>
                    <li>Active: Vertical furling (as diagram) with hyrdraulic, spring-loaded or electric motor driven.</li>
                    <li>Passive: Horizontal furling with yaw</li>
                  </ul>
</div>

            <div class="panel2_inner_blue">
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                Direct: No gearbox needed, requires	a synchronous generator
                Less noise than with gearbox, but more expensive. Prominent example: Enercon wind turbines.</div>
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark"> 
                  <p>Indirect: The rotor drives a gearbox, which drives the asynchronous generator at around 1000rpm. </p>

                Yaw Mechanism

            </div>
            <div class="panel4_inner_blue">
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                  <p>Yaw mechanism moves the nacelle of a horizontal turbine around its tower into the wind when the wind direction changes.</p>
                  <ul>
                    <li>Passive: with a fin attached to the nacelle on the opposite side of the rotor.</li>
                    <li>Active: with a motor</li>
                  </ul>

        <div class="panel2">
            <div class="panel2_header_blue">
                <div class="header_spacer_dark">
                Tower
                </div>
            </div>
            <div class="panel2_inner_blue">
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                  <p>
                    Steel tube</p>
                  <ul>
                    <li>Bending and welding in factory</li>
                    <li>Long experience</li>
                    <li>Steel is expensive. </li>
                    <li>Transporting on the ground subject to size limits 4-5m.</li>
                    <li>Example: REpower</li>
                  </ul>
              </div>
              <div class="inner_spacer_dark"> 
                <p>Lattice tower:</p>
                <ul>
                  <li>Less steel than steel tube.</li>
                  <li>Good for high towers</li>
                  <li>Constructed on-site</li>
                    <li>Requires lots of inspection and on-site labour.</li>
                  <li>Example: SeeBa, Suzlon</li>
                </ul>
              </div>
                <div class="inner_spacer_dark">
                  <p>Concrete tower: </p>
                  <ul>
                    <li>Less use of expensive steel</li>
                    <li>Quality problems with on-site concrete. due to changing weather conditions. Alternative: ship segments from manufactury subject to transport limits.</li>
                    <li>Example: Enercon</li>
                  </ul>
                </div>
